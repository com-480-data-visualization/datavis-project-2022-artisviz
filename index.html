Political context

In the context of the 2022 French presidential election, the subject of minimum wages and working hours has been brought back to the centre of the public debate by candidates and their parties.

Various scenarios are envisioned. Some parties promote an increase in wages without affecting working time while others support overtime work. Still, others think that income should be maintained but with less work, for example by advocating for a 4-day week. These questions are all linked to the same topic: how to increase the general well-being of the population while maintaining productivity and keeping a strong economy?

This project aims to document people by answering the previously listed questions on the international level, through concise but informative data analyses and visualisations. It delivers a factual understanding of the topic and allows a critical view of politicians' promises. Some other questions that this study will help to answer concern the impact of working time on Gross Domestic Product (GDP), inequalities among workers, the productivity of the country, and living standards. The relation between these variables are presented through time and among a selection of countries to give a better of the correlations between them.

This distinction was made to isolate economies of the same type. Indeed, the EU may not have the same dynamics as South Korea, Austalia or the US. It is thus interesting to verify if the EU has a "block" dynamic, if external countries follow this dynamic or if we observe different behaviors.

More down to earth choices were for example, only yearly data has been seleted when quarters or semesters were being suggested. When several categories of people are possible (age or gender), we always chose to keep the "total" category, thus leading to less crowded visualisations. The visualisations

The visualisations are constructed using two tools : D3.js (Data-Driven Documents) and vega-lite. D3 is a visualisation kernel based on javascript (javascript library), and Vega Lite a high-level grammar based on D3. As mentionned previously, Vega Lite allows to acces the source code, but it also grants acces to an online editor if one wants to tweak paramters. The visualisations can also be saved with SVG or PNG format. For each visualisation, a quick "how to navigate" is presented to give all the power to the reader.

Where do people work the most?

How many hours per week do people spend working across OECD? Here is a map of studies OECD countries. Countries are colored according to their number of hours worked: people work the most in red countries, the less in green countries, and black countries are in the middle.

Working times are quite sparse: in some countries people work arounf 1400 hours per year. It is the case for Western and Northern Europe. In Southern and Eastern Europe, as well as North America, Chile and Australia, working times are around 1700 hours per year per capita. Extreme value of our dataset are Russia with around 1900 hours and Mexico with 2100 hours per year.

Work time and income

Working more should result in a higher wage, right? As the saying goes : "make work pay". The following visualisations will put this statement to the test. Let's evaluate people's wealth according to the average hours worked in the country by plotting the average-wage and poverty rate as functions of the hours worked.

To explore the visualisation, you can drag the "Year" cursor or click on it and use your keyboard arrow keys. You can also zoom in and out, select a data point to highlight the curve through the years and make the years appear.

What do these visualisations tell us? First, it appears that the poverty rate is barely dependant of the hours worked. Indeed, we observe limited variations along the x-axis despite the variations along the y-axis. This shows a weak correlation between the two variables. Moreover, the countries do not follow a common behaviour. What can we make from this ? A first interpretation is that the poverty rate is dependant on other major criteria. It would be as foolish to say that working less decreases poverty than to claim the contrary. On the other hand, we observe a common behaviour for the average wages : an increase in average-wages is majorly correlated to a decrease in hours worked per year. That means that there exists factors, both in the EU and in other countries, that lead to this situation. Note that there is a jump of all the points between year 2019 and 2020 : a large decrease in hours worked. This could be linked to the COVID-19 pandemic, since companies have had to adapte the way their employees work as weel as how to count the working hours.

The question now is, does the number of worked has a correlation with any other measure ? We test this hypothesis in the next part.

Work time and productivity

Some say that working less increases your productivity, others claim that productivity comes from more efforts. Let's visualize the relation between the number of hours worked and the GDP per hour worked in the country. You can interact with these visualisations in the same way as the previous ones.

We observe a clear common behaviour among the countries. All seem to direct towards less hours of work for a more important GDP per hours worked. We warn, you, the OECD doesn't specify if their data is adjusted for inflation. In this case, it is essential to cross-check the information with other data sources. In this case, we can have a look at "GDP, 1820 to 2018" from our world in data. There, it is clearly meantionned that the GDP is adjusted for inflation over time. The results are similar to those gotten with the OECD data. We can assure the scenario presented earlier.

These observations let us think that there is a golabl trend where each hour worked produces more and more wealth as time passes.
