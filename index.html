Political context

In the context of the 2022 French presidential election, the subject of minimum wages and working hours has been brought back to the centre of the public debate by candidates and their parties.

Various scenarios are envisioned. Some parties promote an increase in wages without affecting working time while others support overtime work. Still, others think that income should be maintained but with less work, for example by advocating for a 4-day week. These questions are all linked to the same topic: how to increase the general well-being of the population while maintaining productivity and keeping a strong economy?

This project aims to document voters by answering the previously listed questions on the european level through concise but informative data analyses and visualisations. This will give them a factual understanding of the topic and allow a critical view of politicians' promises. Some other questions that the study will help to answer concern the impact of working time on Gross Domestic Product (GDP), inequalities among workers, the productivity of the country, and living standards. To meet our goal, we consider key variables (further detailed in the following) through the past decades.

The data

The data used to produce the next visualisations comes from the The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development ( OECD; French: Organisation de Coopération et de Développement Économiques, OCDE), an intergovernmental economic organisation founded to stimulate economic progress and world trade. You can find all the raw data through the following links :

Some choices have been made to insure readability and generality. For example, only yearly data has been seleted when quarters or semesters were being suggested. WHen several categories of people are possible (age or gender), we always chose to keep the "total" category, thus leading to less crowded visualisation. However, in the latter case, the data has been isolated and can be rendered using the already existing code for the following visualisations.

Where do people work the most?

How many hours per week do people spend working across OECD? Here is a map of studies OECD countries. Countries are colored according to their number of hours worked: people work the most in red countries, the less in green countries, and black countries are in the middle.

Working times are quite sparse: in some countries people work arounf 1400 hours per year. It is the case for Western and Northern Europe. In Southern and Eastern Europe, as well as North America, Chile and Australia, working times are around 1700 hours per year per capita. Extreme value of our dataset are Russia with around 1900 hours and Mexico with 2100 hours per year.
